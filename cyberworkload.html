Cognitive Workload Modeling of Cyber Operators

With Dr. Brett Borghetti, Greg Dye, and Maj. Christina Rusnock

When I first started working at AFIT in 2014, I applied cognitive workload modeling research to the cyber security domain. We analyzed the function of the intrusion detection and reporting systems used by Air Force cyber security operators to find high workload components. Workload, a measure of the total mental effort required to perform a task, can be used to streamline large scale systems. In critical systems, like cyber operations, small changes in parts of the process can drastically decrease workload and increase operator attentiveness and performance.

To model workload, we used IMPRINT, a discrete event simulation and human performance modeling tool. We created a simulation of the intrusion detection process based on descriptions of the process by a subject matter expert. We analyzed the workload results, learning that the system included extremely variable workload, where operators went from high workload to low workload tasks very often. Operators also undergo long periods of constant vigilance. Using this current system, operators must be constantly watching their monitors, like a driver watching for deer on a dark country road. This research has been used by others at AFIT to improve interface design for military cyber operations intrusion detection systems.

After determining the workload related to the high-vigilance task, we decided to create a framework for modeling fatigue related to vigilance tasks. High workload situations are known to cause increased operator fatigue. If operators are experiencing high workload for long periods of time, they will begin to feel the effects of vigilance decrement and fatigue. The IMPRINT community did not have a framework for handling vigilance decrement, so we set out to find a framework of our own. Initially, used we a sustained attention equation found by researchers in the late 1980s which related well to our domain. Eventually we settled on modeling the Psychomotor Vigilance Task, a test used to measure vigilance decrement in times of sleep deprivation. By creating a model instead of using an equation, we created a more general, adaptable, and understandable framework for modeling vigilance decrement and fatigue.
